Abstract

Abstract 1: Genetic polymorphisms play a crucial role in the interindividual variability in response to nutrition, and their identification is essential for understanding personalized nutrition and personalized medicine. In this study, we aimed to construct a local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition by integrating data from the LitVar database and the GWAS catalog dataset. We cataloged and structured data from Medline studies, including title, abstract, keywords, and Mesh terms, related to genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition. Specific sets of Mesh terms related to nutrition physiology, nutrition-related diseases, prevention through diet, and eating behavior were used to extract subsets of genes and polymorphisms. Cross-referencing with the GWAS catalog dataset provided indicative information about possible risk alleles associated with the collected studies.

The constructed database serves as a valuable resource for researchers in the field of nutrition and nutrigenomics, providing a comprehensive repository of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. The integration of data from LitVar and GWAS catalog allows for a more robust and comprehensive cataloging of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition, enhancing the reliability and relevance of the database. The database can be used for further studies on personalized nutrition, nutrigenomics, and personalized medicine, allowing for a deeper understanding of the interplay between genetics and nutrition, and facilitating the development of evidence-based approaches for personalized dietary recommendations and interventions.

Introduction

Nutrition is a critical factor in human health and disease. Emerging evidence suggests that genetic polymorphisms play a significant role in determining the individual's response to different nutrients and dietary patterns. Nutrigenetics, a field that combines nutrition and genetics, seeks to elucidate how genetic variations impact an individual's nutritional requirements, metabolism, and health outcomes. With advancements in high-throughput genomics technologies and the availability of large-scale genetic data, there has been a growing interest in identifying and cataloging genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits.

Currently, various databases and publications contain data on genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition. However, these data are often scattered, diverse in format, and lack a standardized curation process, making it challenging to integrate and analyze them comprehensively. Therefore, there is a need for a curated and consolidated database that integrates these data to facilitate further research in the field of nutrition and nutrigenomics.

In this study, we built a local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition by integrating data from two established sources: the LitVar database, which contains curated information on genetic variations and their functional effects, and the GWAS catalog dataset, which catalogs genetic variants associated with complex traits from genome-wide association studies (GWAS). By leveraging these data sources, we aimed to create [e]a database that could provide a valuable resource for researchers in genomics and bioinformatics to investigate the intricate interplay between genetics and nutrition.

The database includes data from Medline studies, encompassing title, abstract, keywords, and MeSH terms, associated with nutrition-related genetic polymorphism. Specific sets of Mesh terms related to nutrition physiology, nutrition-related diseases, prevention through diet, and eating behavior were used to extract subsets of genes and polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. Cross-referencing with the GWAS catalog dataset provided information about possible risk alleles associated with the collected studies. The database was curated to ensure data quality, consistency, and relevance to nutrition and nutrigenomics research.

There are several limitations to consider in this study. Firstly, the database relies on data from Medline studies, which may be subject to publication bias and may only capture some relevant studies on genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition. Additionally, the data quality and consistency in the database heavily depend on the quality of the original studies and the curation process. Despite efforts to ensure data quality, the original studies’ inconsistencies, errors, and biases may still be present in the constructed database. Furthermore, gene-environment interactions, including interactions with dietary factors, are complex and multifactorial — the current database may capture only some of the possible interactions.[add in future limitations imposed by the selection of reference Mesh terms, results may change with more or less restrictive and/or heterogeneous lists in semantic fields (explained in detail in the methods)]

Moreover, the database is limited to the data available in the LitVar database, GWAS catalog, and other sources used in the study. It may not include all potential genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. Finally, the database may cover only some populations and ethnicities, which could limit its applicability to diverse populations with different genetic backgrounds.

Our database may become a valuable resource for researchers in the field of nutrition and nutrigenomics since it provides a comprehensive repository of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. Integrating data from LitVar and GWAS-catalog allowed for robust and comprehensive cataloging of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition while enhancing the reliability and relevance of the database. The availability of such a curated database will facilitate further investigations into the complex interplay between genetics and nutrition and aid in developing evidence-based approaches for personalized dietary recommendations and interventions. However, limitations such as publication bias, data quality, gene-environment interactions, and population diversity must be considered when interpreting and utilizing the database. Overall, the constructed database provides a valuable resource for nutritionists, researchers, and other stakeholders interested in personalized nutrition and personalized medicine and has potential applications in other areas of research as well.

Database build

Our goal was to build a structured database that aligns genomic data, including genes, single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), and scientific papers from PubMed, with their corresponding Medical Subject Headings (MeSH). This database would facilitate queries using comprehensive and coherent lists of MeSH terms tailored to specific research areas, such as nutrigenetics, enabling the retrieval of pertinent genes and their polymorphisms.

To accomplish this, we performed data integration from both LitVar and PubMed sources.

The LitVar database is a comprehensive and publicly accessible resource that collects information on genetic variations and their associated scientific literature. It aims to bridge the gap between genomic data and the relevant literature by aggregating and organizing information on genetic variants from a wide range of sources [cit 29762787]. PubMed serves as a comprehensive repository of scientific literature in the field of biomedicine and nutrition. Extracting data from PubMed is crucial for various research purposes, such as literature reviews, data mining, and knowledge discovery.

For our purpose, we developed a code pipeline in Jupyter notebook format, which includes the LitVar Application Programming Interface (API) to aggregate all polymorphisms and associated PubMed Identifiers (PMIDs) for each human gene[1],  within the LitVar database. Subsequently, these PMIDs are employed as queries on PubMed to extract the corresponding data in MEDLINE format for each processed gene. To streamline the data collection process, we utilized an NBIB parser, a Python module specifically designed for analyzing MEDLINE-PubMed data (see nbib · PyPI). The collected data were ultimately consolidated into a single CSV file, serving as the core database, where customized sets of MeSH terms can be submitted for retrieval.

Nutrigenetic Database curation

The collected data from LitVar and PubMed were carefully curated using Pandas to ensure data quality, consistency, and relevance to nutrition and nutrigenomics research. Duplicate entries, irrelevant data, and low-quality information were removed to ensure the integrity and accuracy of the database. Data were structured and organized based on standardized formats, including gene names, polymorphism types, genetic variants, functional effects, and associated nutrition-related traits.

Using Pandas, we constructed a relational database (csv-bases database at the moment) in a tabular format to store and manage the curated data. The database was designed with appropriate tables and columns to accommodate the different types of data, including gene names, polymorphism types, genetic variants, functional effects, and associated nutrition-related traits. Data were organized and linked using unique identifiers, such as gene names and polymorphism types, to facilitate easy retrieval and querying of the data.

Database Validation:

To ensure the accuracy and reliability of the constructed database, we conducted thorough validation procedures. Data entries were cross-validated with the original sources, including LitVar and PubMed, to confirm the consistency and accuracy of the data. Any discrepancies or inconsistencies were resolved through careful examination and verification of the original sources. In addition, data quality checks, including data integrity, consistency, and completeness, were performed using Pandas to ensure the reliability and usability of the database.

Database Visualization:

We used Matplotlib, a popular data visualization library in Python, to generate visual representations of the data in the database. This included creating plots, charts, and graphs to visualize the distribution of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits, functional effects of the polymorphisms, and other relevant patterns or trends in the data.

Discussion

The local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition, constructed through integration of data from LitVar, PubMed, and GWAS catalogs, represents a significant contribution to the field of nutrition and nutrigenomics. The availability of this comprehensive database can facilitate personalized nutrition interventions by providing nutritionists and researchers with a valuable tool to design customized diet plans based on individual genetic variations. The use of next-generation sequencing (NGS) or microarrays for genetic testing can provide insights into an individual's genetic makeup, which can then be compared with the cataloged associations between genes, polymorphisms, and nutritional phenotypes.

The integration of data from multiple sources, including LitVar, PubMed, and GWAS catalogs, adds robustness and reliability to the database, as it encompasses a wide range of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. This extensive coverage allows nutritionists and researchers to access a diverse range of information on genes, polymorphisms, functional effects, and associated nutrition-related traits in one centralized database, which can save time and effort in literature searching and data collection.

The availability of graphical representations, such as Figure 1, depicting the distribution of genetic polymorphisms associated with different nutrition-related traits, can provide a visual overview of the data, facilitating data interpretation and analysis. Additionally, the tabular format of Tables 1 and 2 presents the information in a concise and organized manner, allowing for easy data retrieval and comparison.

Furthermore, the cross-referencing of the local database with the GWAS catalog dataset provides additional information on possible risk alleles associated with the collected studies. This cross-referenced data can help nutritionists and researchers in making informed decisions when designing personalized nutrition plans based on an individual's genetic profile.

Overall, the local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition has the potential to significantly impact personalized nutrition and personalized medicine studies. By providing a comprehensive collection of genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits, along with graphical representations and cross-referenced information, this database can serve as a valuable resource for nutritionists and researchers to design personalized diet plans and further advance the field of nutrigenomics.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the construction of a local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition through the integration of data from LitVar, PubMed, and GWAS catalogs represents a significant advancement in the field of genomics and bioinformatics. The availability of this comprehensive database can have wide-ranging applications beyond personalized nutrition, including the investigation of other biological questions related to genetics, nutrition, and health.

One potential application of this method is in the field of personalized nutrition for chronic disease prevention. The database can be utilized to identify specific genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related diseases, such as obesity, diabetes, or cardiovascular diseases, and guide personalized treatment plans. This empowers healthcare practitioners to tailor nutritional interventions based on an individual's genetic profile, resulting in more effective and targeted interventions for disease prevention.

Moreover, the database can also serve as a valuable resource for researchers in the field of nutrigenomics, allowing for further exploration of gene-diet interactions, the identification of novel genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits, and the development of predictive models for personalized nutrition interventions.

However, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of this approach. One limitation is the reliance on available literature and databases, which may be subject to publication bias and may not encompass all possible genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition-related traits. Additionally, the accuracy and reliability of the database depend on the quality and completeness of the data retrieved from various sources, as well as the accuracy of the data structuring and integration process.

Furthermore, the interpretation of genetic polymorphisms and their association with nutrition-related traits may also be influenced by other confounding factors, such as environmental factors, epigenetic modifications, and gene-gene interactions, which are not captured in this database.

In conclusion, while the local database of human genetic polymorphisms associated with nutrition represents a valuable resource for personalized nutrition interventions and other biological questions, it is important to acknowledge its limitations and interpret the results with caution. Further research and validation are needed to fully understand the complex interplay between genetics, nutrition, and health, and to maximize the potential of this database for various applications in the field of genomics and bioinformatics.
